Designing a Room That Glows: The Lighting Behind LUMIÈRE

By Elena Marchetti · 10 March 2026 · 5 min read

← Back to all blogs

A tasting menu is only half the evening. The room carries the other half — so we spent eight months getting the light right before a single dish on the current menu was finalized.

Starting from darkness

Most restaurant lighting starts with a ceiling plan and works down. We did the opposite: we sat at an empty table in a dark room and built the light up from the plate, one fixture at a time, until the food looked the way it does in daylight without the room feeling clinical.

One glow point per table

Every table at LUMIÈRE has a single low, warm light source — nothing overhead. It keeps faces lit, plates lit, and the rest of the room in soft shadow, so thirty tables never feel like thirty tables. Brass fixtures were chosen over anything cooler; brass holds a warmer colour temperature even at low output.

Why warm light changes how food tastes

Colour temperature is doing more work than most diners realise. Cooler light flattens sauces and makes red meat look duller than it is; warm, low light does the opposite — it's why candlelight has always flattered a plate. We measured this obsessively before landing on the final dimmer curve.

A room should disappear a little. You should remember the plate, the person across from you, and the warmth — not the light fixtures.

The details guests don't see

Warm oiled wood tables, low brass fixtures, and a reworked check-linen that nods to the island's old taverna rooms — the reference points were deliberately unpretentious. Fine dining doesn't have to look cold to feel considered.

You can see the room for yourself — reserve a table and come sit under it.
