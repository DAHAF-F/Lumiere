Cooking Over Open Flame: Why Fire Changed Our Menu

By Elena Marchetti · 22 January 2026 · 5 min read

← Back to all blogs

Two years ago, half our kitchen ran on thermostats. Today it runs on wood, embers and a lot more attention — and it's changed how we think about nearly every plate we send out.

Why we lit the fire

A thermostat gives you consistency. Fire gives you something a dial can't: genuine char, shifting smoke, and a heat that changes minute to minute — which forces a cook to actually watch the food instead of trusting a number. That tension is where a lot of our best dishes now come from.

What changes when you cook by feel

Timing stops being a countdown and becomes a conversation with the coals. Fat renders differently over open flame than in an oven — it drips, flares, and bastes the meat on the way down. You can't fully replicate that with electricity, and once you've tasted the difference, it's hard to go back.

The dish that started it: bone marrow

Our roasted bone marrow was the first course we moved entirely onto the fire — split bones, laid straight over embers until the fat blisters and the marrow turns soft enough to spread. We finish it simply: herb salsa verde, flaked sea salt, and grilled sourdough alongside. No sauce competes with what the fire already did.

Fire doesn't let you hide. Every char mark is a decision you made in real time.

What's next on the fire

We're slowly moving more of the menu this direction — mains first, then some of the vegetable courses. You'll find the bone marrow on the current à la carte menu, and it's usually the first thing to sell out.
