Notes on Cypriot Wine: Xynisteri, Maratheftiko and the Island's Cellar

By Elena Marchetti · 14 February 2026 · 6 min read

← Back to all blogs

Cyprus is one of the oldest winemaking regions on earth, yet its indigenous grapes spent decades overlooked on serious wine lists. Ours isn't one of those lists.

An island that never stopped making wine

Vines have grown across the Troodos foothills for thousands of years, largely undisturbed by the phylloxera blight that reshaped much of Europe's wine history. That means Cyprus still farms genuinely old, ungrafted vines — a rarity most wine regions can no longer claim.

Xynisteri: the white that changed our list

Cyprus's signature white grape, grown mostly at altitude in the Troodos mountains. Done well, it's crisp and mineral with a faint waxy note — closer to a cool-climate Old World white than anything tropical. We pour it against our lighter, herb-driven courses.

Maratheftiko: structure without excess

A red grape that's notoriously difficult to grow — it needs a second vine nearby to pollinate at all — but rewards the effort with real structure: dark fruit, firm tannin, and enough acidity to stand up to our open-fire dishes.

A glass of Commandaria

No conversation about Cypriot wine is complete without Commandaria, the island's amber dessert wine, often cited as one of the oldest named wines still produced today. We pour it in small measure alongside dessert — honeyed, raisined, and entirely its own thing.

The best wine on a list isn't always the most famous one. On this island, it's usually the one grown ten minutes from where you're sitting.
