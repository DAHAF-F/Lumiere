Cyprus on the Plate: Cooking with Island Ingredients

By Elena Marchetti · 20 May 2026 · 5 min read

← Back to all blogs

A great menu doesn't begin in the kitchen — it begins in a market, on a boat, or on a mountainside. This is a look at the seasonal Cypriot ingredients and the local producers around Limassol who quietly shape everything we cook.

People are often surprised by how much a small island grows. Cyprus packs an enormous range of microclimates into a short drive: sea-level citrus groves, the cool pine forests of the Troodos mountains, dry inland plains, and a coastline that has fed people here for millennia. For a kitchen built on seasonality, it's a gift.

The market comes first

Most of our week is set by a single morning at the Limassol markets. We don't arrive with a fixed shopping list — we arrive to see what's best, and the menu follows. If the artichokes are perfect, artichokes go on. If the day's catch is a run of red mullet, that becomes the fish course. It's slower and less predictable than ordering from a supplier catalogue, but it's the whole point.

Herbs and greens from the Troodos foothills

Wild fennel, oregano, capers and bitter greens grow across the hills above Limassol. In spring, foragers bring us agrelia (wild asparagus) and tender vine shoots that never travel well enough to buy from abroad. These are the ingredients that make a plate taste specifically of here.

The Mediterranean catch

Our seafood comes off small day boats, not industrial trawlers. That means the menu bends to the weather: sea bream, red mullet, cuttlefish, sea urchin and the occasional prize turbot. We buy the whole catch and design around it, using every part — bones and heads become the stocks and sauces that tie a course together.

An island of wine

Cyprus is one of the oldest winemaking regions in the world, and its indigenous grapes deserve a place on any serious list. We pour crisp, mineral Xynisteri with our lighter courses and structured, ageworthy Maratheftiko with the reds and open-fire dishes. For anyone exploring Cypriot food, the local wine is half the discovery.

The best ingredient is the one that travelled the shortest distance to reach you.

A season on the Cypriot plate

  • Spring: wild asparagus, artichokes, broad beans, green almonds, the first apricots.
  • Summer: heritage tomatoes, courgette flowers, figs, melon, sea urchin.
  • Autumn: grapes and fresh wine must, mushrooms, quince, pomegranate.
  • Winter: citrus, bitter greens, brassicas, slow-cooked pulses and game.

Cooking this way ties us to the island's calendar in a way that never lets the menu get comfortable. It's why no two visits are quite the same — and why the food tastes of Limassol, and nowhere else. You can explore what's on right now, or come see what the market gave us this week.
